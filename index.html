Does Earth's Greening Favor Rich Countries?

Satellite data shows most of the planet greening since 2000. The obvious hypothesis is that this tracks wealth — richer countries, more resources for conservation and agriculture, greener land. We tested that hypothesis against real MODIS NDVI data for 166 countries with complete 2000–2024 coverage, grouped by World Bank income tier. It doesn't hold. Income explains almost nothing about who greened; geography and water do.

FINAL TAKEAWAY

Income doesn't explain who's greening — geography and water do.

From 2000 to 2024, 166 countries with clean MODIS coverage show near-universal greening. We set out to test whether that greening favors wealthy countries. It doesn't: the real driver cuts across income tiers entirely.

ANDGreening is nearly universal

83% of countries are greener in 2024 than their own 2000–2005 baseline, across every income tier.

BUTWealth barely matters

Group means differ by only 1.6 points. Within-group spread is about 10× the between-group spread, and income rank correlates with greening at just −0.31.

THEREFOREGeography is the real signal

Eritrea (low income) and Pakistan (lower-middle income) are the two fastest-greening countries on Earth, driven by dryland and irrigation expansion. Turkmenistan (upper-middle) and Iceland (high income) are the two fastest-browning. Both extremes cut straight across the income ladder.

Why this visualization reveals the takeaway

The globe first shows the surprising global pattern: nearly everywhere is greening. Jumping between individual countries then shows the inversion directly — the greenest gains and the sharpest declines both appear at opposite ends of the income spectrum, not sorted by wealth.

By moving from the global picture to country-level evidence, the visualization lets readers discover the takeaway — income is the wrong lens — rather than simply being told what to believe.
