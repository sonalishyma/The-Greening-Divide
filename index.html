About this project

Mapping Who Is Actually Greening

This project joins NASA MODIS NDVI satellite measurements with World Bank income tiers to test whether richer countries green faster. The analysis covers 166 countries with stable 2000–2024 records and uses each country's own 2000–2005 mean as its baseline.

The expected wealth gradient does not appear. Low-, lower-middle-, upper-middle-, and high-income groups finish within 1.6 percentage points of one another. Variation within each tier is far larger than variation between tiers, and the fastest gains and losses occur at opposite ends of the income ladder.

Why this matters

If greening tracked wealth, environmental recovery would look like a privilege of rich nations. It does not. The result points toward geography, water availability, and land use—and makes recovery look broader and more actionable than the original hypothesis suggested.

How to interpret the maps

On the vegetation maps, higher NDVI generally indicates greener, denser, or healthier plant cover. On the change map, green cells represent an increase in NDVI and orange cells represent a decrease. Spike height represents vegetation intensity on the vegetation maps and the magnitude of change on the change map.

Data

Vegetation comes from NASA MODIS Terra NDVI. Country-level change is measured against a stable 2000–2005 baseline and joined by ISO3 code to World Bank FY2024–25 income classifications. This observational analysis tests association, not a complete causal model.

Step 1 of 5

Testing the wealth hypothesis

Wealthier countries have more resources for conservation, irrigation, and agricultural technology. It seems reasonable to expect them to green faster. The data gives us a chance to test that assumption.

Final takeaway

Income does not explain who is greening—geography and water do.

Across 166 countries with clean MODIS coverage, greening is nearly universal and income tiers barely differ. The strongest gains and losses cut across the income ladder entirely.

And

Greening is nearly universal

83% of countries are greener than their own baseline, across every income tier.

But

Wealth barely matters

Income groups differ by only 1.6 points, while the spread within groups is about 10× larger. Income rank correlates with greening at only −0.31.

Therefore

Geography is the stronger signal

Eritrea and Pakistan are the fastest-greening examples, while Turkmenistan and Iceland are among the fastest-browning. Water, climate, and land use fit the extremes better than wealth.

Why this visualization reveals the takeaway

The globe first establishes the broad greening pattern. The comparison view shows the shift through time, while the change view and country stories reveal that the extremes do not sort by income.

Moving from the global picture to country-level counterexamples lets readers see the original wealth hypothesis fail in the data.
